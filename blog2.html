Building a Bionic Hand out of a Coffee Maker

Previous Next


   On Friday of the conference, Evan Booth demonstrated that the stuff around us has a huge potential for innovation; even with the standard tools you find laying around in your garage. During the climax of his presentation he played a time-lapse of himself dismantling his Keurig coffee maker, only to then use the parts to build a prosthetic hand! It’s hard to see from the image I took of his slide show, but one of his images depicts how the hand is able to bend the fingers in a curled fashion - either starting from the pinky or pointer finger. I felt this added feature was genius and resulted in a much more realistic hand. Also he did play a video of the prosthetic hand grabbing a glass of water and picking it up just fine. I can say that I will never see a coffee maker the same way;) Evan truly validated how many more resources are around us, if we are able to be a little bit more creative and willing to put in some work.


DARPA's Cyber Grand Challenge

Previous Next


   DARPA brought to the table, a never before been done, fully autonomous cyber security ($55 million capture the flag) competition. Where seven super computers competed against one another by probing software for weaknesses, identifying software flaws, and finding and patching those vulnerabilities. All this had to be done while the original function of the software stayed intact and still remained available to the users. Essentially this competition is trying to push towards a future where networks are protected autonomously by computers. For me, this was truly amazing, for this competition was my introduction to this sort of research. I guess what I’m trying to say is that it opened my eyes immensely. I mean the stage was literally like a super organism of tech and fluids, (hundreds of gallons of water where being pumped in and out to keep these super computer cool) thinking at lighting speed to patch software. I felt like I was watching the future of so many things tech unfold before me.


DARPA's CGC website with the Final Event Program.


More!

   Another one of my favorite talks was "101 WAYS TO BRICK YOUR HARDWARE," given by Joe FitzPatrick and Joe Grand (kingpin). They gave insights on how to brick firmware, PCB’s, connectors, IC’s, and also ‘WTF’ scenarios. The most interesting takeaway I had from this talk was the fact that you can fix torn PCB’s, as long as there single layered. I did not know that. As long as you can line up the printed wiring on the board and apply a little solder you can save the board. Also I learned from this talk that your multimeter probes can slip from their position and short something out. This can result in burning the board, ultimately ruining it. Furthermore, the oscilloscope can do the same if improperly connected to ground, yet in this case the oscilloscope can be another very unfortunate loss. Also a never new about 'die analysis' and how it can reveal damages inside chips and that photons can intentionally or unintendedly change the behavior or your IC’s by the photoelectric effect!


   Neurotechx presented a fun, simple DIY project about a brain stimulation device; with schematics! The speaker (neuroscience PhD student whose name wasn’t on the schedule) displayed a prototype of a BSD and talked about its benefits, if used correctly and safely. Apparently when the electrodes of the device are placed correctly on the scalp with no more than 2mA of current applied, the neurons inside you brain will fire easier - stimulating your brain. From studies this can help aid in learning with a certain case showing participents learing 50% faster.
